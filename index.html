The Sound of Australia

What We Buy, What We Love, What We Stream

I grew up hearing the Triple J Hottest 100 every January and assumed Australian music was everywhere. This project tests that assumption against the data. I pulled together almost four decades of ARIA chart records, Triple J listener votes, and Melbourne's live-music venues to look at who actually charts, where the scene is based, and how often Australian artists break through overseas.

Part 1

Where Does Australian Music Come From?

Most ARIA-charting artists come out of Melbourne and Sydney. Melbourne in particular has a dense live scene — the City of Melbourne alone lists 227 registered live-music venues. The map below shows where they sit, suburb by suburb.

The album chart looks more international. The United States, United Kingdom, Canada and Ireland have all supplied large numbers of entries over the decades. Sweden also shows up far more than its size would suggest — largely through ABBA and the Swedish writers behind a lot of modern pop.

227 live-music venues across Melbourne

Each dot is one registered live-music venue in the City of Melbourne, coloured by type. Bars and hotels make up most of them, with a smaller set of theatres and performance spaces. Almost everything clusters in the CBD and along the Fitzroy–Collingwood strip.

Source: City of Melbourne Open Data — Live Music Venues, 2024. data.melbourne.vic.gov.au

Where Does ARIA Album Competition Come From?

Each country is shaded by how many album-chart entries its artists have sent into Australia (darker = more). Australia ranks highest, followed by the USA, UK, Canada and Sweden. Grey countries have no recorded entries. Hover a country for the exact figure.

Source: ARIA Charts — caseybriggs/ARIA-charts, GitHub

🥇 USA Dominates Australian Album Charts

American artists account for 32,046 weeks on the ARIA album chart — more than any other country, Australia included.

Australia is second with 20,666 weeks, which is a lot for a country of 26 million. The UK is third with 15,255.

Ireland comes fifth with 1,846 weeks — a strong result, driven by acts like U2 and The Cranberries. Sweden's 648 weeks also stands out for a country of 10 million, mostly ABBA, Robyn, and the Swedish writers behind a lot of today's pop.

Singles Tell a Different Story

On the singles chart the Australian share is much lower — single digits in recent years, down from about a quarter in the late 1980s.

So Australians still seem to reach for local albums but mostly play international singles on radio and streaming. That gap has been widening since the early 1990s.

Key takeaway

Melbourne's live-music venues cluster tightly in the CBD and along the Fitzroy–Collingwood strip. On the album chart, Australia sits second only to the USA for total weeks on chart.

Part 2

What Australia Buys: The ARIA Story

In the late 1980s, Australian artists held about a quarter of their own singles chart — Kylie Minogue, INXS and John Farnham were regulars in the ARIA Top 50. Over the next three decades that share fell as American and British pop took over.

The streaming era had some big moments — Tones and I's Dance Monkey spent 64 weeks in the top 10 in 2019, and The Kid LAROI topped charts overseas. But these were spikes against a longer decline: by the 2020s, fewer than one in ten chart singles is Australian.

Australian Singles: A Slow Fade from the Charts

Share of the ARIA Top 50 singles held by Australian artists each year. It sat around a quarter in the late 1980s and early 1990s, then drifted into single digits — under one in ten by the 2020s.

Singles vs Albums: Who Owns Which Format?

For each of the 15 most-charted Australian artists, this shows their album weeks (blue) alongside their singles weeks (orange), sorted by combined total. Some artists built their career almost entirely on albums (INXS, Cold Chisel), others mainly on singles (Vance Joy, Guy Sebastian).

Source: ARIA Charts — caseybriggs/ARIA-charts, GitHub

Looking at the data, there seem to be two types of Australian chart career. Some acts — INXS, Cold Chisel — are almost entirely album-driven. Others — Vance Joy, Guy Sebastian — built their careers on sustained singles chart presence. Kylie Minogue is unusual in that she sits near the top of both.

Key takeaway

In the late 1980s, Australian artists held about a quarter of both the singles and album charts. Album share stayed near that level for decades, but the singles share dropped to single digits. The pattern suggests local album buying held up, while singles listening shifted overseas.

Part 3

What Australia Loves: The Triple J Story

The Triple J Hottest 100 is a listener vote held every January. Unlike the ARIA charts, which are based on sales, the Hottest 100 reflects what people actually want to hear. The two rankings often don't agree.

Since 1993 the poll has covered about 4,200 songs across more than thirty years. A small group of artists keeps coming back, while every year also brings artists appearing for the first time. The charts below look at who returns, who makes their debut, and what a long Hottest 100 career actually looks like.

Triple J Royalty: Who Keeps Coming Back

The 25 artists with the most total entries across all Hottest 100 polls. Each cell is one year; darker = more songs in that year's countdown. Artists with long horizontal dark bands kept getting voted in year after year.

Source: Triple J Hottest 100 — ABC Australia, abc.net.au/triplej/hottest100

Fresh Faces vs Familiar Voices

Each poll split into new artists (first time in the Hottest 100) and returning artists (voted in before). The new-artist portion shrinks over time — not because fewer new acts enter, but because the pool of eligible returning artists keeps growing.

Source: Triple J Hottest 100 — ABC Australia

Burn Bright, or Burn Long?

Each dot is one artist with at least three Hottest 100 entries. Positioned left-to-right by how many years their career spanned; bottom-to-top by how many songs they had voted in. Colour shows their best-ever finish — yellow-green is closer to #1. Artists in the upper-right had long, prolific careers; those in the bottom-left appeared briefly and dropped off.

Source: Triple J Hottest 100 — ABC Australia

Key takeaway

Hilltop Hoods have the most Hottest 100 entries of any artist in the data. The share of returning artists in each poll has grown over time — listeners keep voting for acts they already know, which makes the countdown harder to crack for newer names.

Part 4

The Shape of a Hit

The pattern for albums and singles looks quite different once you look at the data by format. Australian acts have held their ground on the albums chart — 267 Australian albums have reached #1 since 1988, including Delta Goodrem's Innocent Eyes (29 weeks at #1) and Savage Garden's debut. On singles the share has steadily fallen. This section traces that split and looks at what actually keeps a song on the chart.

The Album–Singles Gap, Decade by Decade

A summary view by decade. Each row joins Australia's album share (blue dot) to its singles share (orange dot); the connecting bar shows the size of the gap. The 1980s were nearly even — the widest gap came in the 2010s, when album share held at 25% while singles fell to 10%.

Source: ARIA Charts — caseybriggs/ARIA-charts, GitHub

Australia Held the Album Chart, Lost the Singles Chart

The same story shown year by year rather than by decade. Both lines start near 25% in 1988, but albums (blue) hold relatively steady for three decades while singles (orange) drift steadily downward. The year-by-year view also shows volatile individual years that the decade summary smooths over. Press Play to trace both lines from 1988.

Source: ARIA Charts — caseybriggs/ARIA-charts, GitHub

When New Songs Arrive on the Chart, Month by Month

Total new entries onto the ARIA singles chart per calendar month, summed across 37 years. Releases are fairly steady from February through November, with the highest volume around May. December drops sharply — the industry typically slows down over the summer and Christmas period.

Source: ARIA Charts — caseybriggs/ARIA-charts, GitHub

Chart success has two dimensions — peak position and weeks on chart. They are not the same thing. A song can reach #1 and drop off within a month; another can sit around #15 for two years. The chart below looks at which songs actually lasted the longest, and whether they peaked high.

Long-Running Hits Aren't Always #1 Hits

The 15 songs with the most weeks on the ARIA singles chart. Each bar is coloured by peak position: red = reached #1, orange = top 5, blue = #6–10, grey = outside the top 10. Several of the longest-running songs never reached #1 at all.

Source: ARIA Charts — caseybriggs/ARIA-charts, GitHub

Key takeaway

Reaching #1 does not mean a song will stay on the chart for long. Several of the longest-running songs — including Mr Brightside, which peaked at #19 — never made the top 10. Peak position and weeks on chart appear to be largely independent of each other.

Part 5

Australia on the World Stage

Charting at home and charting overseas are very different things. Looking at the US Billboard Hot 100, Australian artists have only managed brief windows of success — the Bee Gees and Andy Gibb in the late 1970s, INXS in 1987, Savage Garden in 1997 and 1999, then a 15-year gap before the next Australian #1 in 2014.

Streaming has changed the picture somewhat. Two Australian songs have held the Spotify Global #1 for extended periods, which would have been nearly impossible under the old radio and sales model. Australia has also been entering Eurovision since 2015, with mixed results.

Every Time an Australian Hit #1 on the US Billboard Hot 100

Every time an Australian artist reached #1 on the US Billboard Hot 100 (1971–2021). Circle size shows how many weeks they held the spot; colour indicates the era. The dashed vertical lines mark the 15-year gap between Savage Garden's last #1 in 1999 and Iggy Azalea in 2014.

Source: Billboard — Down Under on Top (2017), cross-referenced with the Wikipedia List of Billboard Hot 100 number-ones by Australian artists.

Two Australian Songs Have Ruled the Spotify Global #1

Only two Australian songs have ever held #1 on the Spotify Daily Global chart. Both did it for a remarkably long time. Tones and I's Dance Monkey spent 120 days at the top — a record for a non-seasonal song in Spotify history at the time.

Sources: The Music Network — Tones and I, Spotify Global Chart; Guinness World Records — Most days at No. 1 on Spotify Global.

Australia at Eurovision: A Decade of Trying

Australia's results at Eurovision since 2015. Circles show entries that made the grand final; crosses are "did not qualify" (DNQ). The y-axis is final placement — higher means a better result. Dami Im finished second in 2016. From 2021 onwards, Australia has failed to qualify more often than it has made the final.

Source: Wikipedia — Australia in the Eurovision Song Contest.

2 of the 20 Most-Streamed Songs Ever Are Australian

The 20 most-streamed songs on Spotify (as of May 2026), ranked by total streams in billions. Australian songs are highlighted in orange. Stay (#10, 3.94B) and Riptide (#20, 3.69B) are both inside the top 20, with Dance Monkey just outside at #27 (3.45B). Three songs in the global all-time top 30 is a notable result for a country of 26 million.

Source: Wikipedia — List of most-streamed songs on Spotify, as of May 2026.

It took decades of limited overseas success for Australian artists to reach this point. Streaming gave local acts direct access to global audiences — and the numbers show it: three songs in the Spotify all-time top 30, and two Australian tracks each holding the global #1 for months.

Key takeaway

Three of the 50 most-streamed songs on Spotify are Australian — Stay (#10), Riptide (#20), and Dance Monkey (#27). The data suggests streaming gave Australian artists a level of global reach that the old radio and sales model rarely did.
