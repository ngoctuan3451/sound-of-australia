The Sound of Australia

What We Buy, What We Love, What We Stream

Melbourne has one of the most vibrant live music scenes in the world. Australian artists have dominated our own charts — and occasionally broken the world's. But what does the data actually say about how we consume music? This visualisation traces three decades of ARIA chart data, Triple J listener votes, and Melbourne's live music venues to tell the real story of Australian music.

Part 1

Where Does Australian Music Come From?

Australia's music scene is heavily concentrated in two cities. Melbourne and Sydney produce the vast majority of ARIA-charting artists — but Melbourne punches above its weight, hosting over 227 registered live music venues in the CBD alone. The map below shows exactly where that scene lives, suburb by suburb.

Internationally, the ARIA album charts tell a different story. The United States and United Kingdom have dominated Australian charts for decades, with Sweden emerging as a surprising powerhouse through pop exports like ABBA and more recently through the Swedish songwriting machine behind global hits.

Where Melbourne's Live Music Happens

Each hexagon covers roughly 500 metres of Melbourne. Darker hexes pack more registered live-music venues. The clearest signal: the live-music scene is densest along the CBD spine and the Fitzroy/Collingwood corridor, with secondary clusters in Richmond and South Yarra.

Where Does ARIA Album Competition Come From?

Darker countries have sent more album chart entries into Australia. Australia itself ranks highest — followed by the USA, UK, Canada and Sweden. Grey countries have no chart entries recorded. Hover any country for exact figures.

Source: ARIA Charts — caseybriggs/ARIA-charts, GitHub

🥇 USA Dominates Australian Album Charts

American artists account for 32,046 weeks on the ARIA album charts — more than any other country, including Australia itself.

Australia ranks second with 20,666 weeks — impressive for a country of 26 million. The UK comes third with 15,255 weeks.

Sweden's 648 weeks is remarkable for a country of just 10 million people — a testament to Swedish pop's global dominance through ABBA, Robyn and modern hitmakers.

Singles Tell a Different Story

On the singles chart, Australian artists drop to just 8.4% — a dramatic fall from their album dominance.

This gap suggests Australians actively seek out local albums but passively consume international singles through radio and streaming — a pattern that shifted significantly in the streaming era after 2018.

Part 2

What Australia Buys: The ARIA Story

For most of the past 37 years, Australian artists struggled to break into their own singles charts. The ARIA Top 50 was dominated by American and British acts — local artists might crack the top 10 occasionally, but sustained chart presence was rare. Then something changed around 2018.

The streaming era arrived, and with it came a new path to chart success. Tones and I released Dance Monkey in 2019 and spent 64 consecutive weeks in the Australian top 10. The Kid LAROI became the first Australian-born artist to top charts globally. Suddenly, Australian artists owned their own charts in a way they never had before.

Singles vs Albums: Who Owns Which Format?

For each of Australia's 15 most-charted artists, the chart pairs their album weeks (blue) against their singles weeks (orange). Bars are sorted by combined total — some artists are clearly album acts, others live on the singles chart.

Source: ARIA Charts — caseybriggs/ARIA-charts, GitHub

Some Australian artists are album artists — INXS, Cold Chisel, Kylie Minogue — who built loyal audiences through cohesive records. Others are singles machines — Tones and I, The Kid LAROI — whose streaming numbers far outpace album sales. The album era belonged to the 1980s and 1990s; the singles era belongs to streaming.

Part 3

What Australia Loves: The Triple J Story

Every January, millions of Australians vote in the Triple J Hottest 100 — the world's largest music poll. Unlike the ARIA charts, which measure sales, the Hottest 100 measures love. The results are often very different. Songs that top ARIA are often absent from Triple J, and vice versa.

Since the first poll in 1993, the Hottest 100 has featured roughly 4,200 song entries across more than thirty annual countdowns. A small group of artists shows up year after year, while every January brings a fresh wave of debutants. The next three charts trace who returns, who arrives, and who builds a real career out of it.

Triple J Royalty: Who Keeps Coming Back

A heatmap of the 25 most-played artists across every Hottest 100. Each cell is one year; darker cells mean more songs in that year's poll. Look for the long horizontal bands — those artists never went away.

Source: Triple J Hottest 100 — ABC Australia, abc.net.au/triplej/hottest100

Fresh Faces vs Familiar Voices

Stacked-area split of every poll into new artists (never charted before) and returning artists. The orange band shrinks over time as the cumulative pool of past artists grows — a healthy sign that voters reward consistency, not just novelty.

Source: Triple J Hottest 100 — ABC Australia

Burn Bright, or Burn Long?

Each dot is one artist. X = years between their first and last appearance. Y = total songs voted in. Colour = best position ever reached. Long-career legends sit to the upper right; one-hit wonders cluster bottom-left.

Source: Triple J Hottest 100 — ABC Australia

Part 4

The Shape of a Hit

Australia's strongest chart story has always been told on the albums chart. Since 1988, 267 Australian albums have reached #1 — from Delta Goodrem's Innocent Eyes (29 weeks at #1) to Savage Garden's debut (19 weeks) to John Farnham's Age of Reason. The chart below plots every one of them.

Australia's Year-by-Year Album Chart Domination

A simple yearly view: how many weeks per year was the ARIA #1 album by an Australian artist? Some years (2003, 2007) Aussie acts held #1 for over half the year; in other years they barely touched it.

Source: ARIA Charts — caseybriggs/ARIA-charts, GitHub

Chart success has two dimensions — how high a song peaks and how long it stays. They are not the same. A song can hit #1 for one week and vanish. Another can sit at #18 for two years and quietly outsell the lot.

Long-Running Hits Aren't Always #1 Hits

The 15 songs that spent the most weeks on the ARIA singles chart. Bars are coloured by how high each song actually peaked — red = #1, orange = top 5, yellow = top 10, grey = never made top 10. A grey bar means the song was a slow burn that never went to #1.
