The Sound of Australia

What We Buy, What We Vote For, What We Stream

I grew up hearing the Triple J Hottest 100 every January and assumed Australian music was everywhere. This project checks that idea with data: almost four decades of ARIA chart records, Triple J listener votes, Melbourne live-music venues, and global streaming results. The story moves from Australia's local music base, to what charts at home, to what listeners vote for, and finally to how often Australian artists break through overseas.

Part 1

Local Venues, Global Album Competition

The story starts with place. Melbourne has a dense live-music base: the City of Melbourne lists 227 registered live-music venues, mostly clustered around the CBD and inner north.

But the charts are not only local. ARIA is Australia's main music chart, and its album chart is strongly international. The second map zooms out from Melbourne to show which countries supply the artists who spend the most weeks on Australia's album chart.

Melbourne music venues cluster in the city centre

Each dot is one registered live-music venue in the City of Melbourne. Colour shows the type of venue. Most venues are bars and hotels, and the densest cluster is in the CBD and along the Fitzroy-Collingwood strip.

Source: City of Melbourne Open Data — Live Music Venues, 2024. data.melbourne.vic.gov.au

USA artists lead Australia's album chart

Each country is shaded by how many weeks its artists spent on Australia's album chart. Darker countries contributed more chart weeks. The USA ranks first, followed by Australia and the UK. Grey countries have no recorded entries, and rows without country metadata are excluded from the map.

Source: ARIA Charts — caseybriggs/ARIA-charts, GitHub

USA Dominates Australian Album Charts

American artists account for 32,046 weeks on the ARIA album chart — more than any other country, Australia included.

Australia is second with 20,666 weeks, which is a lot for a country of 26 million. The UK is third with 15,255.

Ireland comes fifth with 1,846 weeks — a strong result, driven by acts like U2 and The Cranberries. Sweden's 648 weeks also stands out for a country of 10 million, mostly ABBA, Robyn, and the Swedish writers behind a lot of today's pop.

Singles Tell a Different Story

On the singles chart the Australian share is much lower — single digits in recent years, down from about a quarter in the late 1980s.

So Australians still seem to reach for local albums but mostly play international singles on radio and streaming. That gap has been widening since the early 1990s.

Key takeaway

Australia has a strong local music base, especially around Melbourne's inner-city venues. But the album chart is global competition: Australia sits second only to the USA for total weeks on chart.

Part 2

What Australia Buys: The ARIA Charts

Now we move from where music is made to what Australians buy and stream on the ARIA charts. In the late 1980s, Australian artists held about a quarter of the Top 50 singles chart. Over the next three decades, that local singles share fell as American and British pop took up more space.

The charts below move from the big pattern to the artists behind it: first the falling local share, then the album-versus-singles gap, then the Australian artists with the longest chart careers.

Australian singles became rarer after 1990

This chart shows the share of Top 50 singles by Australian artists each year. Around 1990, roughly one in four charting singles was Australian. By the 2020s, it was usually fewer than one in ten.

Source: ARIA Charts — caseybriggs/ARIA-charts, GitHub

Albums stayed more Australian than singles after the 1980s

Each row summarises one decade. The teal dot shows Australia's album-chart share and the coral dot shows its singles-chart share. The 1980s are the closest decade, with singles slightly ahead; the 2010s show the widest gap.

Source: ARIA Charts — caseybriggs/ARIA-charts, GitHub

Australian albums stayed stronger than singles

This shows the album-versus-singles gap year by year. Both lines start near 25% in 1988. Album share stays fairly steady, while singles share falls. Press Play to trace the pattern from 1988 onward.

Source: ARIA Charts — caseybriggs/ARIA-charts, GitHub

Australian singles artists by chart weeks

This ranks Australian artists by total weeks on the ARIA singles chart. Drag across the year strip to focus on a period. With no selection, it shows the all-time leaders, led by Kylie Minogue on 370 weeks.

Tones and I led Australian singles for three years

Each line is one Australian artist. Rank 1 means the artist spent the most weeks on the singles chart that year. The chart shows Tones and I leading for three years before The Kid LAROI and Vance Joy moved up.

Source: ARIA Charts — caseybriggs/ARIA-charts, GitHub

Kylie Minogue leads albums and singles

For each major Australian artist, teal shows album-chart weeks and coral shows singles-chart weeks. Some artists are mostly album acts, such as INXS and Cold Chisel. Others are mostly singles acts, such as Vance Joy and Guy Sebastian.

Source: ARIA Charts — caseybriggs/ARIA-charts, GitHub

Looking at the data, there seem to be two types of Australian chart career. Some acts — INXS, Cold Chisel — are almost entirely album-driven. Others — Vance Joy, Guy Sebastian — built their careers on sustained singles chart presence. Kylie Minogue is unusual in that she sits near the top of both.

Key takeaway

In the late 1980s, Australian artists held about a quarter of both the singles and album charts. Album share stayed near that level for decades, but the singles share dropped to single digits. The pattern suggests local album buying held up, while singles listening shifted overseas.

Part 3

What Australia Votes For: Triple J

The Triple J Hottest 100 is an annual listener vote for favourite songs. Unlike the ARIA charts, which are based on sales and streaming, the Hottest 100 shows what Triple J listeners choose. The two rankings often tell different stories.

The full dataset contains about 4,200 Hottest 100 entries: 3,300 entries from regular annual polls between 1993 and 2025, plus 900 entries from special all-time countdowns. For the time-series charts below, I use only the 33 regular annual polls so the year-to-year comparisons stay consistent.

Triple J listeners keep voting for these artists

This shows the 25 artists who appear most often in regular Hottest 100 countdowns. Each row is one artist and each column is one year. Darker cells mean that artist had more songs voted into that year's countdown.

Source: Triple J Hottest 100 — ABC Australia, abc.net.au/triplej/hottest100

More Hottest 100 artists have been there before

Each year is split into new artists appearing in the countdown for the first time and returning artists who had appeared before. Over time, more artists are able to return, so familiar names take up more of the countdown.

Source: Triple J Hottest 100 — ABC Australia

Few artists stay in the Hottest 100 for years

Each dot is one artist with at least three Hottest 100 entries. Artists further right stayed relevant for more years. Artists higher up had more songs voted in. Colour shows their best result, with brighter colours closer to #1.

Source: Triple J Hottest 100 — ABC Australia

Key takeaway

Hilltop Hoods have the most Hottest 100 entries of any artist in the data. The share of returning artists in each poll has grown over time — listeners keep voting for acts they already know, which makes the countdown harder to crack for newer names.

Part 4

When Songs Arrive and What Keeps Them Around

After looking at who gets onto the chart, this section asks what happens to individual songs. First, it shows when new songs usually enter the ARIA singles chart. Then it separates two ideas that are easy to mix up: how high a song peaks, and how long it stays around.

New songs slow down in December

This adds up all new songs entering the ARIA singles chart by month. New entries are steady from February to November, peak around May, and drop sharply in December when the music industry slows over summer and Christmas.

Source: ARIA Charts — caseybriggs/ARIA-charts, GitHub

Long hits do not always reach number 1

Chart success has two dimensions: peak position and weeks on chart. They are not the same thing. These are the 15 songs that stayed on the ARIA singles chart the longest. Bar length shows total weeks on the chart; colour shows the song's best position.

Source: ARIA Charts — caseybriggs/ARIA-charts, GitHub

Key takeaway

Reaching #1 does not mean a song will stay on the chart for long. Several of the longest-running songs — including Mr Brightside, which peaked at #19 — never made the top 10. Peak position and weeks on chart appear to be largely independent of each other.

Part 5

Australia on the World Stage

Charting at home and charting overseas are very different things. Looking at the US Billboard Hot 100, Australian artists have only managed brief windows of success — the Bee Gees and Andy Gibb in the late 1970s, INXS in 1987, Savage Garden in 1997 and 1999, then a 15-year gap before the next Australian #1 in 2014.

Streaming has changed the picture somewhat. Two Australian songs have held the Spotify Global #1 for extended periods, which would have been nearly impossible under the old radio and sales model. Australia has also been entering Eurovision since 2015, with mixed results.

Australian US number 1 hits came in waves

Each circle is an Australian artist's #1 single on the US Billboard Hot 100. Bigger circles stayed at #1 for more weeks. The dashed lines mark the 15-year gap between Savage Garden's last #1 in 1999 and Iggy Azalea in 2014.

Source: Billboard — Down Under on Top (2017), cross-referenced with the Wikipedia List of Billboard Hot 100 number-ones by Australian artists.

Two Australian songs reached global number 1 on Spotify

Only two Australian songs have reached #1 on Spotify's daily global chart. Both stayed there for a long time. Tones and I's Dance Monkey spent 120 days at the top, a record for a non-seasonal song at the time.

Sources: The Music Network — Tones and I, Spotify Global Chart; Guinness World Records — Most days at No. 1 on Spotify Global.

Australia's best Eurovision result was second place

This shows Australia's Eurovision results since joining in 2015. Circles reached the grand final; crosses did not qualify. Higher on the chart means a better result. Dami Im's second place in 2016 remains Australia's strongest result.

Source: Wikipedia — Australia in the Eurovision Song Contest.

Two of Spotify's top 20 songs are Australian

This ranks the 20 most-streamed songs on Spotify as of May 2026. Australian songs are highlighted in coral. Stay and Riptide are both inside the top 20, while Dance Monkey is just outside at #27.

Source: Wikipedia — List of most-streamed songs on Spotify, as of May 2026.

The data tells a clear story. For most of the past 50 years, Australian artists could dominate at home — on the album chart especially — but international breakthrough was rare and brief. Streaming removed the gatekeeping of radio and physical distribution. Two Australian songs held the Spotify Global #1 for a combined 197 days. Three sit in the all-time top 30. That is a structural shift, not a fluke.

Key takeaway

Three of the 50 most-streamed songs on Spotify are Australian — Stay (#10), Riptide (#20), and Dance Monkey (#27). The charts and the venues, the sales and the votes, all point to the same conclusion: Australian music has always had a strong local base. What streaming did was finally let that base reach the world.

Data Sources

  • ARIA Charts (1988–2025): caseybriggs/ARIA-charts on GitHub — 95,800 weekly chart rows for singles and albums, with Australian-artist flags and country of origin.
  • Triple J Hottest 100 (1993-2025): ABC Australia - 3,300 regular annual-poll entries, plus 900 all-time/special countdown entries.
  • Melbourne Live-Music Venues (2024): City of Melbourne Open Data Portal — 227 registered venues with location, type, and address.
  • Basemap geometry: Victoria locality polygons and Natural Earth countries via the FIT3179 Vega-Lite repository.
  • Eurovision Australia entries (2015–2025): Wikipedia, Australia in the Eurovision Song Contest.
  • Australian #1s on the US Billboard Hot 100: Billboard's Down Under on Top (2017) and the Wikipedia List of Billboard Hot 100 number-ones by Australian artists.
  • Spotify Daily Global #1 records: Guinness World Records; The Music Network.
  • Spotify all-time most-streamed songs (top 50): Wikipedia — List of most-streamed songs on Spotify, snapshotted May 2026.

Method note: ARIA country totals use known-country album-chart rows; rows with missing country metadata are excluded from the world map. Historic ARIA singles rows had incomplete Australian-artist flags, so singles-share values use a conservative lower-bound repair based on reliably tagged album rows and post-2019 singles rows. Spotify all-time rankings are a May 2026 snapshot.
